# Statement of Purpose

I am Tarunika Ponnusamy, a final-year B.Tech student in Instrumentation and Control Engineering at the National Institute of Technology, Tiruchirappalli (NIT Trichy), where I rank 1st among 120 students with a CGPA of 9.71. Growing up in a home where my father freelanced as an electronics consultant, I was naturally drawn to building small systems and experimenting with platforms such as Arduino and ESP32. While I initially viewed robotics as an extension of electronics, my perspective shifted in college when I began to see robots as dynamic systems that sense, move, and interact with the world. This shift became more concrete when I witnessed the consequences of an elderly fall and encountered simple pressure-sensor-based systems that detect weight shifts and alert caregivers in real-time. The impact of such minimal yet thoughtful designs demonstrated to me how system behavior directly affects safety and trust, motivating me to focus on designing robots whose motion and interaction are predictable, reliable, and centered on human needs.

Joining NIT Trichy after clearing the Joint Entrance Examination (JEE), one of India's most competitive national exams, placed me in an environment that valued depth, rigor, and independent thinking. Being surrounded by peers who were equally driven pushed me to look beyond whether a system merely works and to ask how and why it behaves the way it does. This approach shaped my first significant project, a power-assisted wheelchair, where I designed and tuned a gain-scheduled PID controller to achieve smoother and more natural motion during terrain transitions. I further explored behavior-critical control during my internship at the Defence Bioengineering and Electromedical Laboratory (DEBEL), DRDO, Bangalore, where I worked on sensor-less torque estimation and control for BLDC motors in a knee-assist exoskeleton. These experiences highlighted how closely dynamics, control design, and user comfort are intertwined in assistive systems, and how small changes in modeling or feedback can significantly impact safety and trust.

As my interests matured, I sought experiences that exposed robotic systems to more complex and less ideal conditions. Through my work in the robotics club at my college, I built a soft robotic gripper, which introduced me to compliant actuation, and later developed a 12-DOF quadruped for search-and-rescue applications, where gait generation, inverse kinematics, and maintaining stability on uneven terrain became central concerns. These hands-on projects, supported by coursework in control systems, nonlinear control, robotics, and embedded systems, strengthened my understanding of modeling, feedback, and system behavior beyond idealized settings.

This perspective deepened during my internship at the University of Southern California, where I worked under Professor Feifei Qian on granular locomotion as part of the NASA-funded LASSIE project, which involved collaboration across multiple universities. Being in the RoboLAND Lab, I regularly participated in lab meetings, technical discussions, and research talks, where different groups shared experimental results, modeling approaches, and failures as openly as successes. Observing how researchers compared results across platforms and iteratively refined experiments helped me understand how robotic behavior in complex environments is studied in practice, as well as how theoretical models are constantly tested and revised through physical experiments. This experience has shaped my approach to research, with a greater emphasis on experimental rigor and system-level understanding, rather than focusing solely on isolated results.

My bi-copter project at NIT Trichy then pushed me to work closely with the stability and control challenges of an underactuated aerial system. Focusing on how disturbances, asymmetric thrust, and payload variations affect flight behavior, I implemented and compared controllers such as PID, full-state feedback, and LQR/LQG to study their impact on robustness and stability margins. This work is supported by the Ram and Thaila Ramanujam Foundation Gold Scholarship, which has allowed me to pursue independent undergraduate research in systems and robotics. My ongoing work on the REHABOT project at the Idiap Research Institute, under the supervision of Professor Sylvain Calinon, has added a complementary perspective centered on physical human-robot interaction. Developing virtual guides using signed distance fields and impedance-based control has made it clear how sensitive interaction behavior can be to minor force variations, and how essential it is for controllers to remain stable, compliant, and predictable when a robot works directly with a human.

These experiences made it clear to me that I want to continue my graduate training in an environment that values understanding robotic behavior through dynamics, control, and physical interaction. This is what draws me to the MS program in Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of California, San Diego, and specifically to the Intelligent Systems, Robotics, and Control (ISRC) track. The way robotics and control research at UCSD spans both theoretical work and experimental systems closely matches how I have approached robotics through my own projects. I am particularly interested in research at the Advanced Robotics and Controls Lab, where Professor Michael Yip's work on modeling and control for medical and assistive robotic systems aligns with my experience in physical human–robot interaction, and in the Existential Robotics Laboratory led by Professor Nikolay Atanasov, whose focus on robustness and uncertainty-aware robotic systems resonates with my interest in reliable system behavior under non-ideal conditions. Overall, UCSD's research environment feels well-suited to the kind of careful, system-focused robotics research I want to pursue.

When I look back on my undergraduate years, what stands out most is how my initial enthusiasm for engineering gradually evolved into a clearer sense of direction. I have come to realize that I am most drawn to problems where a strong understanding of system dynamics and control directly shapes how a robot behaves and how people experience it. That clarity has guided the projects I have chosen and the way I approach them, and it is why I want to continue studying robotics in a research-focused environment. At UC San Diego, I see an opportunity to deepen this understanding through rigorous training in robotics and control, while maintaining close connections to real systems and real-world interactions. I hope to carry this approach forward by developing robotic systems whose behavior is reliable, safe, and grounded in a clear understanding of the systems that govern them.
