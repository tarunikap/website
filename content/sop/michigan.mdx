# How I Got Here

I did not grow up being the obvious standout in every room I entered. Instead, I learned early how it feels to sit across from someone stronger or more experienced and still have to perform. Competitive chess became the space where I first encountered this. I began playing tournament chess in kindergarten and later represented Tamil Nadu at national championships, as well as India at the Commonwealth Chess Championship. As academic demands increased during high school, continuing at the next level became difficult due to limited access to advanced coaching in Coimbatore, which would have required regular travel of nearly 500 kilometers, at a time when online training options were minimal. Although I stepped away from competitive chess, the discipline, composure, and habit of steady self-improvement it instilled have remained central to how I approach challenges.

Equally influential was the culture around chess. After games, players would sit together analysing lines, sharing ideas, and pointing out better moves without hesitation. Winning was not a moment to gloat, and losing was not a reason to disappear. Both were starting points for learning. That openness shaped my perspective on education itself. I learned early that progress happens most naturally in communities where people are willing to admit what they do not know and help others improve. That mindset stayed with me when I transitioned into engineering. Growing up and studying in India meant learning in environments where resources were not always abundant, and opportunities were unevenly distributed. In college, especially through my robotics club, we often had limited access to ideal hardware or perfect setups. We learned to work with what was available, often improvising and refining systems incrementally. Those constraints shaped how I think and work, teaching me to value patience over speed, consistency over shortcuts, and collaboration over individual effort.

Over time, I found myself drawn to roles that focused on learning alongside others. I mentored juniors, broke down complex ideas into simpler steps, and stayed engaged even when progress was slow. Through my college community, I also participated in outreach efforts that introduced students from government schools in and around the Tiruchirappalli region to hands-on problem-solving and basic engineering tools. Many of these students had strong curiosity but limited exposure, which reinforced my belief that learning environments matter just as much as individual ability.

These experiences motivated my decision to pursue graduate study in an environment that values collaboration, curiosity, and steady intellectual growth rather than purely competitive outcomes. This is what draws me to the University of Michigan. The emphasis on shared learning, interdisciplinary interaction, and strong student communities reflects the kind of spaces where I have learned best, from chess tournaments to engineering labs. Pursuing a graduate degree here feels like a natural continuation of this journey. I want to grow in an environment where questions are welcomed, learning is collective, and progress is achieved through sustained engagement. Michigan offers that, and it is where I see myself developing both academically and personally.
