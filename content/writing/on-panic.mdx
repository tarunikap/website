# on panic

***left alone for long enough,***  
***i learned to work only in extremes.***

College didn't really teach me this habit.  
I picked it up on my own, somewhere between freedom and zero supervision.

Most of the time, I exist like barren land.  
Quiet. Still. No visible activity. It looks like nothing is happening, because nothing is.

Then a deadline gets close.  
And suddenly I'm a pressure cooker. Heat everywhere. Panic-fuelled efficiency. Things getting done at a speed that feels slightly alarming.

The funny part is, the deadline was never a surprise.  
It didn't move. It didn't hide. It just waited while I pretended it wasn't urgent enough yet.

Being on my own made this feel normal.  
No one checking in. No one telling me when to start. No structure except the final date. So I taught myself to trust panic. Stress became the trigger. Urgency became the work ethic.

And it works.  
Tasks get done. Submissions go through. From the outside, it looks functional. Reliable, even.

But the funny thing is, not everything in life responds to panic.  
Your body doesn't. Your mind definitely doesn't.  
They don't speed up just because a deadline is close.

Some things refuse to cooperate with this system.  
Health doesn't sprint. Rest doesn't show up on command.  
No amount of last-minute urgency fixes that.

But the cycle keeps going.  
Not because it's healthy.  
Just because it's familiar.

Barren land. Pressure cooker.  
Rinse. Repeat.

Not ideal at all.  
Extremely deadline compatible.
